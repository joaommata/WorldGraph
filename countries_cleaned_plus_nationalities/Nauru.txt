 was first settled by Micronesians at least 3,000 years ago, and there is evidence of possible [[Polynesians|Polynesian]] influence. Comparatively little is known of  prehistory, although the island is believed to have had a long period of isolation, which accounts for the distinct language that developed among the inhabitants. There were traditionally twelve clans or tribes on , which are represented in the twelve-pointed star on the [[flag of |country's flag]]. Traditionally, [[Nauruans]] traced their descent [[Matrilineality|matrilineally]]. Inhabitants practised [[aquaculture]]: they caught juvenile [[milkfish]] (known as ''ibija'' in [[ language|]]), [[acclimatization|acclimatised]] them to freshwater, and raised them in [[Buada Lagoon]], providing a reliable food source. The other locally grown components of their diet included [[coconut]]s and [[pandanus tectorius|pandanus fruit]]. The name "" may derive from the  word ''{{lang|na|An√°oero}}'', which means 'I go to the beach.'

In 1798, the [[British people|British]] sea captain [[John Fearn (whaler)|John Fearn]], on his trading ship ''Hunter'' (300 tons), became the first Westerner to report sighting , calling it "Pleasant Island" because of its attractive appearance. From at least 1826, Nauruans had regular contact with Europeans on whaling and trading ships who called for provisions and fresh drinking water. The last [[whaler]] to call during the [[Age of Sail]] visited in 1904.

Around this time, [[desertion|deserters]] from European ships began to live on the island. The islanders traded food for alcoholic [[palm wine]] and firearms. The firearms were used during the 10-year [[ Civil War]] that began in 1878.

After an [[Anglo- Declarations about the Western Pacific Ocean|agreement with Great Britain]],  annexed  in 1888 and incorporated it into the  Protectorate for administrative purposes. The arrival of the [[Germans]] ended the civil war, and kings were established as rulers of the island. King [[Aweida|Auweyida]] was the most widely known. Christian missionaries from the [[Gilbert Islands]] arrived in 1888. The  settlers called the island "Nawodo" or "Onawero". The Germans ruled  for almost three decades. [[Robert Rasch]], a  trader who married a 15-year-old  girl, was the first administrator, appointed in 1890.

Phosphate was discovered on  in 1900 by the prospector [[Albert Fuller Ellis]]. The Pacific Phosphate Company began to exploit the reserves in 1906 by agreement with , exporting its first shipment in 1907. In 1914, following the outbreak of World War I,  was captured by  troops. In 1919, it was agreed by the [[Allies of World War I|Allied and Associated Powers]] that [[George V|George V of the ]] should be the administering authority under a League of Nations mandate. The  Island Agreement forged in 1919 among the governments of the , , and  provided for the administration of the island and extraction of the phosphate deposits by an intergovernmental [[British Phosphate Commission]] (BPC). The terms of the [[League of Nations]] mandate were drawn up in 1920.

The [[1948  riots]] occurred when  [[guano]] mining workers went on strike over pay and conditions. The  administration imposed a [[state of emergency]] with [[Native Police]] and armed volunteers of locals and  officials being mobilised. This force, using [[Submachine gun|sub-machine guns]] and other firearms, opened fire on the  workers, killing two and wounding sixteen. Around 50 of the workers were arrested; two of them were bayoneted to death while in custody. The trooper who bayoneted the prisoners was charged but later acquitted on grounds that the wounds were "accidentally received." Rehabilitating the island was seen as financially impossible.
