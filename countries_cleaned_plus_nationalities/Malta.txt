===Prehistory===
{{See also|Megalithic Temples of |ƒ¶al Saflieni Hypogeum|Gƒßar Dalam}}

 has been inhabited from at least {{Circa|6500 BC}}, with the arrival of [[Mesolithic]] hunter-gatherers likely originating from Sicily. Discoveries at [[Latnija Cave]] led by the  archaeologist Eleanor Scerri included the remains of hearths, stone tools and an abundant and diverse range of animal bones. These included indigenous red deer that are now extinct, fish and marine mammals, as well as abundant edible marine [[gastropods]]. To arrive on , these hunter-gatherers had to cross around 100&nbsp;km of open water, documenting the longest yet-known sea crossing by hunter-gatherers in the Mediterranean. The extinction of the [[Hippopotamus melitensis|dwarf hippos]], [[Cygnus falconeri|giant swans]] and [[Palaeoloxodon falconeri|dwarf elephants]] has historically been linked to the earliest arrival of humans on . However this seems unlikely since recent work suggests these animals went extinct many thousands of years before the arrival of the first people, and no such animals were found in association with the earliest known Mesolithic hunter-gatherers. The claim for an earlier occupation of the island by [[Neanderthal]]s is widely rejected. <sup>Supplementary Information</sup> [[Early European Farmers|Neolithic Farmers]], also originating from Sicily, are thought to have arrived on the islands by around 5400 BC. Prehistoric farming settlements dating to the [[Early Neolithic]] include the [[Gƒßar Dalam]] cave site in the uppermost layers associated with domesticated animals. The Neolithic population on  grew [[cereals]], raised livestock and, in common with other ancient Mediterranean cultures, worshipped a [[mother goddess|fertility figure]].

[[File:Ggantija Temples, Xaghra, Gozo.jpg|thumb|[[ƒ†gantija]] [[megalith]]ic temple complex]]
A culture of [[megalith]]ic temple builders then either supplanted or arose from this early period. Around 3500 BC, these people built some of the oldest existing free-standing structures in the world in the form of the megalithic [[ƒ†gantija]] temples on [[Gozo]]; other early temples include those at [[ƒ¶aƒ°ar Qim]] and [[Mnajdra]]. The temples have distinctive architecture, typically a complex [[trefoil]] design, and were used from 4000 to 2500 BC. Tentative information suggests that [[animal sacrifice]]s were made to the goddess of fertility, whose statue is now in the National Museum of Archaeology in Valletta. Another archaeological feature of the  Islands often attributed to these ancient builders is equidistant uniform grooves dubbed "cart tracks" or "cart ruts", which can be found in several locations throughout the islands, with the most prominent being those found in [[Misraƒß Gƒßar il-Kbir|Misraƒß Gƒßar il-Kbir ("Clapham Junction")]]. These may have been caused by wooden-wheeled carts eroding soft limestone. The culture apparently disappeared from the islands around 2500 BC, possibly due to famine or disease.

After 2500 BC, the  Islands were depopulated for several decades until an influx of [[Bronze Age]] immigrants, a culture that [[Cremation|cremated]] its dead and introduced smaller megalithic structures called [[dolmen]]s. They are claimed to belong to a population certainly different from that which built the previous megalithic temples. It is presumed the population arrived from [[Sicily]] because of the similarity of  dolmens to some small constructions found there.

During the [[First Punic War]], the island was conquered after harsh fighting by [[Marcus Atilius Regulus]]. After the failure of his expedition, the island fell back in the hands of Carthage, only to be [[Capture of  (218 BC)|conquered again]] during the [[Second Punic War]] in {{nowrap|218 BC}} by the [[Roman consul]] [[Tiberius Sempronius Longus (consul 218 BC)|Tiberius Sempronius Longus]].  became a {{lang|la|[[Civitas|Foederata Civitas]]}}, a designation that meant it was exempt from paying [[tribute]] or the rule of [[Roman law]], and fell within the jurisdiction of the [[Roman province|province]] of [[Sicilia (Roman province)|Sicily]]. Its capital at [[Mdina]] was renamed {{lang|la|[[Melita (ancient city)|Melita]]|italic=no}} after the  and Roman name for the island. In the second century, Emperor [[Hadrian]] upgraded the status of  to a {{nowrap|[[municipium]]}} or free town: the island's local affairs were administered by four [[duumviri|duumvirs]] and a municipal senate, while a Roman [[procurator (Roman)|procurator]] living in [[Mdina]] represented the [[proconsul]] of Sicily. In {{nowrap|AD 58,}} [[Paul the Apostle]] and [[Luke the Evangelist]] were shipwrecked on the islands. Paul remained for three months, preaching the [[Early Christianity|Christian faith]]. In 395, when the [[Division of the Roman Empire|Roman Empire was divided for the last time]] at the death of [[Theodosius I]], , following Sicily, fell under the control of the [[Western Roman Empire]]. During the [[Migration Period]] as the [[fall of the Western Roman Empire|Western Roman Empire declined]],  was conquered or occupied a number of times. From 454 to 464 the islands were subdued by the [[Vandals]], and after 464 by the [[Ostrogoths]]. In 533, [[Belisarius]], on his way to conquer the [[Vandal Kingdom]] in North Africa, reunited the islands under Imperial ([[Byzantine Empire|Eastern]]) rule. Little is known about the [[Byzantine |Byzantine rule in ]]: the island depended on the [[Sicily (theme)|theme of Sicily]] and had  governors and a small  garrison. While the bulk of population continued to be constituted by the old, [[Latin Church|Latinized]] dwellers, during this period its religious allegiance oscillated between the Pope and the [[Ecumenical Patriarchate of Constantinople|Patriarch of Constantinople]]. The Byzantine rule introduced [[|]] families to the  collective.  remained under the [[Byzantine Empire]] until 870, when it was conquered by the [[Arabs]].

=== Empire and the Second World War===

In 1814, as part of the [[Treaty of Paris (1814)|Treaty of Paris]],  officially became a part of the  Empire and was used as a shipping way-station and fleet headquarters. Between 1915 and 1918, during the [[First World War]],  became known as ''the Nurse of the Mediterranean'' due to the large number of wounded soldiers who were accommodated there. On 7 June 1919, the  public rioted in response to a [[cost-of-living crisis]];  troops eventually managed to suppress the riots, killing four in the process. The event, known as [[Sette Giugno]] ("7 June"), is commemorated every year and is one of five National Days. Until the [[Second World War]],  politics was dominated by the [[Language Question ()|Language Question]] fought out by [[Italophone]] and [[Anglophone]] parties.

Before the Second World War, Valletta was the location of the Royal Navy's Mediterranean fleet headquarters; however, despite [[Winston Churchill]]'s objections, the command was moved to [[Alexandria]], , in 1937 out of fear that it was too susceptible to air attacks from Europe.
